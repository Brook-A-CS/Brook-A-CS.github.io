Internet security revolves around HTTPS, which has slowly become the standard to help protect users. The two primary purposes of a HTTPS connection are to enhance the user’s security and privacy through sending the website data through a type of connection called Secure Sockets Layer (SSL) or Transport Layer Security (TLS). Both SSL and TLS describe the same process of an encrypted connection that cannot be broken. At first, HTTPS was expensive and therefore used sparingly, however as it became cheaper over time, many websites began to use it for the whole website. This has caused issues with institutions who wish to see what users are doing on their computers. These include the government, such as the FBI, as well as private employers and schools who have had a harder time monitoring those they oversee. To get around these security measures, they use an HTTPS Proxy Appliance to create man in the middle attacks. These proxies impersonate Certificate Authorities (CA). Certificate Authorities check if a website is who it says it is, after sufficient evidence such as physical address, public phone numbers, etc. (Gibson, 2013) After they receive sufficient information, a CA will sign off the website’s security certificate. In order to monitor a user, an institution will impersonate a CA through an HTTPS Proxy Appliance, after that, The Proxy Appliance will be the “man in the middle” and connect the website to the user through its own servers. After this point, the owner of the Proxy has access to every way the user interacts with the website and can log everything for a complete history of everything the user has done, or it can enforce whatever policies it chooses such as content filters for certain words.

SSL Interception

SSL interception cannot be prevented, however you can still keep yourself protected. The first thing you must do is determine whether or not you are being intercepted, which can easily be done in any modern browser. I will only mention how to do it in Chrome, however the process is similar in other browsers. First you need to find the fingerprint value of a website on your network, to do this: Navigate to the padlock at the left-end of the URL bar, click it, then click “Connection is secure,” after this click “Certificate is valid,” which will open a menu. This menu is determined by the operating system you are running, however in Windows, you need to navigate to the details tab, then scroll to the bottom where it says Thumbprint and click it. This will display the hexadecimal fingerprint/thumbprint value. Now you must compare this to the same site’s fingerprint on another network. While this can be done physically by going to another network (such as your home network instead of a school/work one) it is easier and more convenient to use this site (gcr.com) which will get the fingerprint of a website through its own servers. If the fingerprint on your personal browser, and the one found on this website match, it means that your network does not have any SSL interception. If this site ever goes down, or its integrity is ever in question, you will have to find a second network to test on, but keep in mind that if the fingerprints do not match, you will have to consider whether the original network you tested or the secondary one is the one being injected.

Hashing

The fingerprint you see here is just a number in hexadecimal format. This is called a hash; a hash is created by taking all of the data within a file (in this case the website) and running it through an algorithm until it comes out to, usually, a hexadecimal value with a fixed length. A good hash algorithm, such as SHA-3 is developed so that when anything in the original file changes, down to as small as a single bit, it will create a “snowball effect,” where the hash will be significantly different. (Gibson, 2013) A good hash will be easy to convert from a normal value into a hash value, however, it should be very difficult to take the hash value and create the original file. (Schneier, 2004) As technology and computing power improves older hashes will become easy and quick to decode which will lead to the creation of new hashing algorithms, however just as computing power increases exponentially, so do new hashing algorithms.

False Positives/Negatives

This method of detection is not fool-proof and a matching/mis-matching fingerprint might have other explanations. Very large websites, such as Google, Youtube, Amazon, etc, might have multiple fingerprints depending on your location in order to load the webpage as fast, therefore, mis-matching fingerprints could just mean that your location and grc.com have different certificates which are closest to it. To get around this you should try multiple websites and attempt on smaller websites (such as gcr.com itself). False mis-matches are not the only issue, there can also be matching fingerprints even if you are being SSL intercepted. This can occur because whoever is doing the intercepting has blacklisted whatever site you are using to test the fingerprint. This means they will not know what you are doing on that specific website; however, all other websites are still vulnerable. (Gibson, 2013) The best way to get around this is, once again, to test multiple websites, however make sure to try and stick to small websites.

Privacy

My personal belief on whether schools, governments, ISP’s, or employers should have the right to eavesdrop on you is that they should not be able to, however with certain exceptions. Schools, workplaces, and ISP’s should never be able to spy on you, because any spying they do will be to further themselves and not the general public, therefore it is not fair to expect people to give up a significant part of their privacy in order to benefit another entity. The government however should have the ability to begin eavesdropping on you through a warrant, just like any physical area you own. This will allow government institutions to strike a balance between protecting people's freedoms and protecting people’s wellbeing. This stance brings up the argument that, in order for a government to spy on its citizens after a warrant, it will have to develop and maintain the ability to spy on its citizens at all times which can easily be abused. While it does mean that the government can spy on its citizens at any point as long as it is not caught, and let's be honest even if/when it is caught nothing will change, I still believe that this is the best possible compromise since almost every government in the world has the ability to track its citizens online, and so it is unrealistic to expect any of them to reduce this ability. Expecting them to suddenly stop having the ability to spy on its citizens would be equivalent to asking a nation that has developed nuclear weapons to stop production and disarm all of their current ones, which is safe to say impossible.
